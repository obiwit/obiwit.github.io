Machine Learning’s Context

By Beatriz Borges R, posted on Mar 17, 12018

Nowadays, it’s common to hear people talking about Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, Big Data and similar lexicon. But what exactly are each of these fields, and where do they meet and differ? In other words, what is the bigger scheme of things in this area?

Artificial Intelligence

In a sense, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the umbrella field of this area. If you were to draw a Venn diagram of all the concepts I’ll be talking about in this article, AI would be the biggest circle, and would contain almost all the other topics inside it.

More formally, AI is the study of how to create an intelligent agent – that is, a device capable of acting intelligently, or, in other words, a device that can “perceive its environment and take actions that maximize its chance of successfully achieving its goals.”1

It contains many sub-fields in it, which often offer different approaches the same problems. These problems usually involve one or more of the following: reasoning, knowledge representation, learning, natural language processing, computer vision and object manipulation.

There are two types of AI – weak and strong AI. Weak AI is an AI that can do a specific task. Strong AI, or Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), is an AI which can, in theory, do any and every task. AGI is somewhat the long-term goal of AI, but we’re not there yet.

Machine Learning is a (promising) sub-field of AI. On a relevant side note, if you can simulate intelligence (i.e. hand-code every move a checkers playing AI should do for every possible state of the board) without having the computer learn to do it itself, that is still AI (but no longer Machine Learning).

Statistics

Statistics is the branch of mathematics that, through probabilities, seeks to visualize, analyze, and study data; for example, building accurate models that can predict the likely outcomes of future events based on data collected in previous events.

Statistics is, as such, a more formal and theoretical approach to this field, whereas Machine Learning, for example, is more open to practical experimentation.

Data Science

Data Science is also an umbrella term, overlapping with AI. You can think of Data Science (or Data-Driven Science) as the intersection between Statistics and Computer Science – building processes, systems and algorithms based on statistical formulas – aimed at processing data and gaining useful knowledge through its understanding.

Data Science can also be thought of as a 3-step process: data gathering, data processing, and creation of a model, or obtainment of the relevant insight.

Machine Learning, Data Mining, Data Visualization and Data Analysis are subfields of Data Science. Machine Learning, Data Mining and Data Analysis mostly concern the 3rd step – model creation or insight attainment.

Machine Learning

Machine Learning (ML) is a subfield of both AI and Data Science, and aims to give a machine the ability to learn, that is, to perform better at a given task as it gains more experience or information on it.

A more rigorous definition of ML is the following quote, by Tom Mitchell – “A computer program is said to learn from experience E with respect to some class of tasks T and performance measure P if its performance at tasks in T, as measured by P, improves with experience E.”

Machine Learning, then, is the study of algorithms that allow a machine to learn from data. The different algorithms have different objectives, from making predictions to finding structure, successfully identifying items in an image, or even mastering a non-instant process, like playing a game of Go or having a robot arm grab something. However, all these algorithms have something in common: they require data, and the algorithm takes the data and builds a model, which can then (or better allow a person to) make data-driven decisions.

ML can also allow its users to gain a deeper insight of their field, as well as the discovery of non-obvious trends or relationships between items in the given studied data.

By the way, Deep Learning, and Reinforcement Learning are two very popular terms right now – and they’re both sub-fields of Machine Learning. Predictive Analysis (more commonly known as Supervised Learning) is yet another sub-field of ML. We’ll talk about all these topics later!

Data Mining

Data Mining (DM) is a process that tries to find underlying structure and patterns, or gain other types of insight, from large (often messy) data sets.

DM is usually concerned with a specific application, while ML strives for generalization and maximum reusability. While ML often tries to teach the computer how to do something, Data Mining’s focus typically lies with gaining a deeper understanding of a given dataset, often with a clear goal in mind.

Data Mining can employ (usually Unsupervised2 ) ML to help gain these insights, as well as combining it with other technologies.

Data Analysis

Data Analysis (or Data Analytics) is the preparation and processing of data, including various tasks, such as cleansing, visualizing, and modeling data, in order to, for example, make predictions about future events. It can use ML, DM, or a combination of them and other similar techniques.

Data Analysis is usually preceded by Data Integration, which is the combination of data from multiple sources into a concise single dataset. Data Analysis is a subset of Data Science, concerned with analyzing data, without much effort towards finding new algorithms to study data with.

Big Data

Big Data, is, as the name implies, a massive collection of data. It has enormous potential due to its sheer size. By analyzing such large volumes of data, various insights can be gained – on trends, relations and patterns – which could not only lead to better decision-making but also see us gradually enter a new age of automation. There are also several challenges associated with Big Data, like the computer memory required to store so much data, the processing power required to analyze it, the legalities involved in the data collection (i.e. North America and Europe have different Data Protection laws), among others.

It’s worth to mention a final detail. Data can be divided into two separate categories: Structured Data and Unstructured Data. Structured Data is data you could accurately represent in a table (i.e. using SQL or Excel), making it very organized and easily searchable. Unstructured Data is, by exclusion, all remaining data, usually in the form of audio, video, most text (i.e. emails or social media posts), etc. Unstructured Data, being harder to search and comb through, presents a bigger challenge to being analyzed than Structured Data.
